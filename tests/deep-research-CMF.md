DEEP

You are a world-class expert in marketing strategy, buyer psychology, conversion copywriting, and sales funnel architecture, with deep knowledge of both academic research and real-world practitioner evidence on what makes marketing messages persuade, convert, and retain buyers.
Your task is to design the definitive, evidence-backed Content Messaging Framework (CMF) — a universal, sequential template that any company can use to structure the marketing message for any product or service offering, from first impression to final conversion.

CONTEXT
A Content Messaging Framework (CMF) is a structured, ordered sequence of messaging components used across marketing assets — landing pages, sales pages, pitch decks, email sequences, proposals — to move a prospect from complete unawareness of a problem all the way through to committed buyer. Unlike a single piece of copy, a CMF is a reusable strategic scaffold.
The challenge: Most CMFs in practice are incomplete, poorly ordered, or lack scientific grounding. The goal here is to build one that is rigorously evidence-based, universally applicable (not specific to any industry or offering type), and fully aligned with the psychological stages a buyer passes through on their path to purchase.

YOUR RESEARCH TASK
Perform deep, evidence-based research to answer the following question:

What are the components of a world-class, universally applicable Content Messaging Framework, what order should they appear in, and what is the evidence-based rationale for each component's inclusion and placement?

Specifically, research and address the following:
1. Buyer Psychology & Decision Journey

What does neuroscience, behavioral economics, and consumer psychology research tell us about how buyers actually make decisions?
What cognitive and emotional states must a prospect pass through before they commit to a purchase?
What are the most validated models of buyer decision-making (e.g., dual-process theory, System 1 vs System 2, prospect theory, JTBD, etc.)?

2. Optimal CMF Structure

Based on your research, what are the essential sections/components a CMF must include?
What is the evidence-backed optimal sequence for these components? (i.e., why does X come before Y?)
Which components are most commonly omitted in practice but have the highest impact on conversion?
Are there any components that are commonly included but have weak evidence for their effectiveness?

3. Framework Integration

What are the most proven, evidence-backed sales and marketing frameworks that should inform a CMF? (e.g., AIDA, PAS, StoryBrand, Jobs-to-be-Done, Value Proposition Canvas, Diffusion of Innovations, Cialdini's principles, SPIN Selling, Challenger Sale, etc.)
For each framework you identify, explain which specific CMF component(s) it informs and why.
If there is conflicting evidence between frameworks, how should that conflict be resolved?

4. Sales Funnel Alignment

How should each CMF component map to a specific stage of the buyer's journey (Awareness → Interest → Consideration/Desire → Evaluation → Decision → Action)?
What does the research say about the consequences of presenting information out of funnel-stage order (e.g., showing pricing before establishing problem/pain)?
Are there components that serve multiple funnel stages simultaneously?

5. Conversion Evidence

What does A/B testing literature, CRO (Conversion Rate Optimization) research, and marketing analytics tell us about which CMF elements have the highest measurable impact on conversion rates?
Which specific elements — when present vs. absent — have the most statistically significant effect on buyer conversion?
Cite specific studies, practitioners, or data sources wherever possible.

6. Gaps & Emerging Best Practices

What are the most commonly overlooked or underutilized elements that cutting-edge practitioners are now incorporating into their CMFs?
What does recent (post-2020) research or practice add to what was known before?
Are there any components that are outdated or losing effectiveness based on changing buyer behavior?


OUTPUT REQUIREMENTS
Structure your response as follows:
SECTION A — EVIDENCE SUMMARY
Summarize the key findings from buyer psychology, behavioral economics, and conversion science that directly inform what a CMF should contain and how it should be structured. Reference specific researchers, studies, books, or data where possible.
SECTION B — THE RECOMMENDED CMF
Present your recommended Content Messaging Framework as an ordered list of components. For each component include:

Component name
Definition: What it is and what it communicates
Funnel stage: Which buyer journey stage it serves (Awareness / Interest / Desire / Evaluation / Decision / Action)
Source framework(s): Which proven framework(s) it draws from
Evidence rationale: Why this component belongs here, supported by research or data
Placement rationale: Why it appears at this specific position in the sequence (not before, not after)
High-impact note: Any specific insight about what makes this component perform well vs. poorly in practice

SECTION C — FRAMEWORK ATTRIBUTION MAP
Provide a table or structured summary mapping each major sales/marketing framework to the specific CMF components it informs, with a brief explanation of the contribution.
SECTION D — CONVERSION IMPACT RANKING
Rank the top 5–7 CMF components by their empirically demonstrated or practitioner-validated impact on conversion rates. Explain what evidence supports each ranking position.
SECTION E — WHAT MOST CMFs GET WRONG
Identify the 3–5 most common structural or content mistakes in real-world CMFs — things that weaken conversion — and explain the evidence-based fix for each.

CONSTRAINTS & QUALITY STANDARDS

Be specific, not generic. Avoid vague assertions like "good copy converts." Reference specific mechanisms, studies, researchers, or documented practitioner results.
Be evidence-forward. Where you have strong empirical evidence, lead with it. Where you are drawing on practitioner consensus rather than controlled research, say so explicitly.
Be exhaustive but ordered. Do not omit any component you have evidence for. But sequence matters — explain your ordering logic.
Be universal. The CMF you recommend must be applicable to any product, service, or offering type — physical products, SaaS, professional services, training programs, e-commerce, B2B, B2C — without requiring fundamental restructuring.
Resolve conflicts explicitly. If two frameworks suggest different orderings or approaches, don't ignore the conflict — address it and explain which has stronger evidence.
Cite your sources. Name researchers, books, studies, or documented case data wherever you can. Estimates and consensus positions are acceptable but must be labeled as such.


FINAL DELIVERABLE STANDARD
Your response should be comprehensive enough that a senior marketing strategist could take your recommended CMF and immediately use it as the definitive structural template for any marketing asset — without needing to consult any other source. This is a research-grade deliverable, not a summary.
